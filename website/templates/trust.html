Hobson's Conduit Trust

A look at the trust over the years

Objectives

The Trust dates from 1632 and is responsible for upkeep of Hobson’s Brook from Nine Wells to the Conduit Head, and its underground branches through Cambridge. Today, the trust is focused around five guiding objetives.

Trust Objectives

  1. To preserve this historic chalk stream for its ecological and public amenity value.
  2. To provide water supply to the Trumpington Street runnels, Emmanuel, Christ’s and Pembroke Colleges and the University Botanic Garden.
  3. To preserve the character of the Brook as an example of early civil engineering.
  4. To maintain an appropriate volume of water flow and an acceptable water quality.
  5. To secure an adequate level of funding to support these objectives.

History

The water supply was named after Thomas Hobson, a local entrepreneur and carrier of goods between Cambridge and London. When he died in 1631, Thomas Hobson left funds in his will to set up a Trust to look after the ground on both sides of the ‘new river’ and the water course itself.

Although the ‘new river’ provided fresh water for the townspeople and colleges, it never completely cleared the King’s Ditch, which was eventually covered over. Today, the stream, the Conduit head and the runnels as well as the supply to the botanic garden and the college pools all remain a vital, visible part of Cambridge heritage.

Cambridge citizens and our many visitors also benefit from the wide variety of plant and animal wildlife that this high quality green corridor supports. The Trustees hope that these goals are very much in the spirit generated by Thomas Hobson and the original founders.

Volunteer for the trust

The Hobson’s Conduit Trustees rely on volunteers to help keep the banks, paths and the brook clear and help with assessing different species in the BioBlitz. This makes sure that water flow and its environs are maintained so everyone can enjoy this ancient waterway.

What do our volunteers do?

In the last months, volunteers have removed discarded ironwork, various items of clothing and even an odd shoe! This is in addition to the usual bottles, cups, cans and pieces of fallen timber, which regularly find their way into the water.

Our volunteers have also helped in identifying animal and plant species found along the stream during the Hobson’s Conduit Bioblitz. (need link to internal page about Bioblitz here). This will help quantify the wildlife in and around the brook over the next few years to be sure that the present broad diversity of species is maintained for the future.

The volunteers also enjoy the social aspect of the work with refreshments during and after work on the brook. Trustees and interested specialists provide briefings about Hobson’s Conduit, its history and development and wildlife in this important heritage area.

We are very grateful to our volunteers but are always looking to recruit. If you would like more information please contact Toni Birkin

The existing Trustees

Howard Slatter

Howard took his degree at Cambridge, and had a career teaching mathematics in schools, latterly back in Cambridge. He lives in Trumpington, where he is involved with the Local History Group. His interests include birdwatching and family history. He has been a Trustee since 2008 and became chairman in 20XX.

Graeme Minto

Graeme read Engineering at Cambridge University and in 1971 joined Cambridge Consultants and became involved in the emerging technology of Ink Jet Printing. With CCL support, he founded Domino Printing Sciences plc at Bar Hill. Graeme has also been a local magistrate, church treasurer, churchwarden, and a trustee or director of several local organisations. He is now an active member of the Cambridge Rotary Club and has been a Trustee in 2001.

Simon King

After studying Biochemistry and Microbiology at Sheffield University, Simon's early career was in the Army culminating in command of an independent sub-unit in Cyprus. His second career was in recruitment, working for Norman Broadbent and PA Consulting, and setting up his own Cambridge-based company providing services for science, technology and engineering companies. From 2005, Simon has provided support to various initiatives across Addenbrooke’s hospital leading to his current management role on campus development at CUHP. He became a Trustee in 2017.

Max Field

Max was born in Cambridge, and studied at Charing Cross Hospital. He worked in Yorkshire, Oxford, London and lastly in Glasgow as a rheumatologist with an interest in medical education. Upon retirement, Max and his family moved to Cambridge and he now lives near Nine Wells. Max became a Trustee in 2015.
